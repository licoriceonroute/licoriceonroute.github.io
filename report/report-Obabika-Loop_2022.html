Obabika Loop

August 24th - 29th 2022

Preface

After being away for so many years I really wanted to come back to Canada in the summer, especially to visit the canadian shield. I've always loved it, but living away for so long has given me a new appreciation for the rocks, the lakes, and the solitude. My favourite way to experience it has always been from the water, so what better activity to do than a canoe trip with some friends.

After doing a lot of research, and timing it with everyone's schedules, we settled on the easily accessible, and beginner-friendly Obabika Loop in the Temagami Canoe Area of southeastern Ontario.

Our group consisted of Hudson, Graeme, Stefan, and myself. Hudson and Graeme had done a canoe trip together at summer camp before, so were experienced with the demands of multi-day canoeing.
Stefan and I had gone on a multi-day Kayak trip in Georgian Bay in 2016, the last time I was here in the summer. so he also knew what he was getting into.

Everyone was looking forward to the trip, and we planned everything online so we would be prepared when I arrived.

We borrowed some important packing gear from Rolf, and a second tent from Wendy, so lots of thanks goes out to them.

Day 0 | Arrival | Tuesday 23.08.2022
Drive: 446km Toronto - Temagami

Even though we had done a test-pack the night before, we did a final pack with all the food and left after Hudson's work to drive from Toronto to Smoothwater Outfitters.
Shortly after North Bay we got a pressure warning light in the car. We got to the outfitter around 01:30 and Hudson kindly announced our arrival with a friendly honk. We pitched the tents in the dark as quickly as possible and got to sleep around 02:00.

After deeming all the permanent seating in the station to be inadequate, we set up our camping chairs next to an out of the way window and had a relaxing wait with Wendy offering up some Jam up the Mash and a third of the birthday cake allowance we had for the week.

Day 1 | Flat | Wednesday 24.08.2022
Drive: 32km
Paddle: 13.44km
Portage: 1650m



Waking up on time for breakfast we entered the lodge for eggs, toast, fruit, bacon, and coffee at 08:00. At the table we met a physics teacher and his family visiting their eldest daughter at smoothwater and had a very nice conversation with him. We got outfitted with our canoes, bear barrel, and other accessories before driving a little ways down the highway to the Red Squirrel logging Road. The road proved to be rough and consisted primarily of sharp gravel as well as larger rocks, the fine dust immediately coated everything outside of the car, and the bushes and flowers on the embankments were all white with cloying powder.



The tire that had had the low pressure warning the night before and had seemed fine this morning gave out around four kilometres into the road. Changing it for a spare on the side of the road was challenging with the logging trucks going past, some stopped to ask if we were alright while others rumbled by, barely slowing down, causing us to stick our heads under the canoes to avoid the thick clouds that obscured the road for half a minute at a time.




After changing the ruined tire we took a very careful drive to the put-in. The parking lot was 1.12km from the beach on Sandy Inlet, and the portage took us first down a rough road, impossible for us to drive down, and then a placid forest path. Most of the cars in the parking lot nearer the beach were serious offroaders, but some, like a Honda Odyssey, showed their drivers had either incredible talent or complete disregard for damage.



We paddled to the Pickerel Bay portage in around an hour and there found a path characterized by a treacherous 20m long section of broken stones and jagged rocks making it the most difficult part of this portage, we were sure however that there would be more difficult portages to come.

Having completed our first water to water portage we quickly crossed the North Arm of Lake Temagami and picked our way through the narrow passages into southern Sharp Rock Bay, intent on finding a campsite there. With all of them either occupied or inaccessible we decided to head north towards the Sharp Rock Portage and search for a site among the cluster of three up there.

Taking the easternmost we had a great dinner of steak, snap peas, onions, and potatoes cooked over an open fire.



The drab sky eventually gave way to a beautiful overcast sunset and as the sun went down the stars slowly crept out from behind the clouds. We fell asleep to the loon calls and the lapping of waves on the rocks.

Day 2 | Beauty | Thursday 25.08.2022
Paddle: 13.92km
Portage: 120m


Expecting rain I woke up at 05:00 to a clear and starry night; the air was cool but we all stayed warm enough in our sleeping bags. The situation at 07:00 looked very different however, with clouds looming in from the west.



We tried to get a fire going as quickly as possible and to get the tents down before the rain started, not soon enough as it turned out, as a drizzle started just as breakfast was getting underway. We took the dry inners into a dry sack and stuffed the wet flys in another.



A delicious breakfast of scrambled eggs with tomatoes in a pot and bacon fried on a sheet of foil followed, and after we had cleared up breakfast the rains stopped as we departed towards the Sharp Rock Portage.

A lift over a beaver dam was required to get into the most westerly inlet of Sharp Rock Bay just before the Sharp Rock Portage, which lived up to its name. Luckily it was very short and we quickly continued into Diamond Lake, quickly fishing three leeches out of the bottom of the canoe before heading off.



Diamond Lake proved to be very pretty and completely deserted apart from a float plane dropping people off at a cabin. Paddling Diamond Lake without the headwind of which I had been warned seemed like appropriate compensation for the wet morning.




We took a small detour into the Bob Lake Conservation Reserve; deciding against paddling in, we made BLT wraps with the leftover bacon from breakfast. The next leg would be our last of the day as we headed west to look for a campsite, but not before Stefan soaked Hudson with the splash from a well thrown rock.

The campsite of the day was high up on the rocks giving us a great view of the surrounding scenery.



A small jumping rock was found and the swimming was great in the deep water off the eastern side, Stefan and Graeme tried their luck fishing, but to no avail. A beaver also greeted us, swimming by twice in the afternoon sun.



Dinner would be hotdogs, peppers, and zucchini grilled over the fire, and we sat out by the flames until the rain started up. It strengthened steadily until it settled into a downpour and we fell asleep to the sound of rain on the tents and the highlights of the Jays game on the radio.

Day 3 | Peace | Friday 26.08.2022
Paddle: 17.65km
Portage: 850m


The rain let up in the early morning, but it left the tents and the ground damp. We were dry enough though and crawled out to face a blustery and overcast morning.

We made a quick breakfast of oats with dried fruit and nuts, as well as some hard boiled eggs, before taking the tents down and striking out onto the cold water. The agreed morning swim would have to wait but seeing two loons on the lake right next to the boats was a nice bonus.

Paddling the rest of the west arm of Diamond Lake, the sun made its first appearance of the day around noon. We made our way into the final muddy bay but it took a while to find the portage route as the path split off in two directions



This first 400m portage proved to be the most difficult of the trip, consisting of many large boulder fields and several rock “staircases” that took some careful foot placement to navigate. The portage may have been difficult but it was also very picturesque, having us lug our gear across the lip of a cliff overlooking a forest and the stony creek beyond.

The paddle across Lain Lake was very short and after pulling our canoes up we found a much more agreeable portage along a disused logging road and a short, clear forest path. We were passed on these by two other canoes, the popular consensus being that it was okay as we had more stuff.




After paddling south through Pencil Lake we pushed through a small creek and found ourselves on the beachy northern shore of Lake Wakimika. We struck out onto the water and soon took lunch on an island near the eastern shore. I had to grab a leech off my toe, adding to the various other foot injuries. A lunch of chorizo, cheddar, and tomato on wraps would keep us fueled to dinner, and more importantly, the Wakimika river.

The river turned out to be a winding waterway in two parts, the first requiring a series of liftovers and precise paddling to navigate, slowing our pace significantly. The environs were very pretty, with tall trees on either side casting shade on the water and the reeds and the slow, clear stream pulling us south. After a small lake to break up the river it widened for the second half and let us take a much more relaxed approach. We also saw the most wildlife yet. A small and a large sandpiper, herons, owl, beaver, kingfisher, and a crane.






Leaving the confines of the river we made our way south through Obabika Lake with an increasingly strong tailwind speeding us along to our campsite: an island with two great pitches and a flat stone shore giving an expansive view to the east.

It was getting dark as we started our dinner of pasta with pesto and we decided to take the telescope out as we ate to admire the stars. This proved impossible as clouds rolled in once again, and as we washed our dishes and headed to sleep we hoped that a clear night sky would present itself before the trip was over.

Day 4 | Joy | Saturday 27.08.2022
Paddle: 13.84km
Portage: 470m


After the clammy cold of the previous morning the weather today was uplifting. We awoke to a clear sunny sky and got to making our oats, intent on breaking camp earlier than the day before.



We paddled south on Obabika Lake, bypassing the 900m portage in favour of a 400m and 100m. The longer portage turned out to be a winding forest path, the kind of trail you hope for going out on a hike.



After crossing a small unnamed lake (I’m going to call it Golden Cove) we found ourselves in a lillypad-filled Obabika Inlet, and back on Lake Temagami.

Golden Cove and Obabika Inlet proved to be one of the more scenic paddles we’d done with the high forested hills on all sides speckled with bands of yellow-turning birch trees



Intent on keeping the day short, we wanted to stop before the next 500m Portage and got off the water around 14:15 to make camp on an exposed peninsula with a panoramic view of the lake and the cliffs opposite.

Standing on the best jumping rock over a very deep part of the shore also proved to be the first time we spotted any fish larger than a minnow, but all we could really make out were their shadows contorting in the depths.

A late lunch of pasta aglio e olio followed the swims and we were happy to simply enjoy the sunshine after so many overcast hours.

Fishing led to a few intensive nibbles and a couple of grabs but nothing taken ashore.
As the sun went down we sat out looking across the bay, drinking bourbon out of a bottle of perrier and enjoying each other’s company as the sun went down.




Night fell and playing poker with skittles for chips lasted only a few hands (resulting in a crushing defeat for me and a mouthful of sweaty skittles for everyone else) before we were out stargazing, in awe at the darkness of the sky.

Day 5 | Easy | Sunday 28.08.2022
Paddle: 15.51km
Portage: 580m


We woke again to a sunny morning and to a bald eagle circling the camp. Graeme tried his luck at fishing once again but the required party seemed uninterested. After again preparing oats and fruit for breakfast we broke camp and paddled past a semi-permanent cabin with some docks out front before reaching the portage to Devil Bay, a muddy 500m forest trail which Hudson decided to trapse through barefoot carrying a canoe.
The blustery paddle out of Devil Bay to Ferguson Bay across the North Arm of Lake Temagami was long and fraught with waves. The constant up and down was too much for one of the seat struts which decided to take the rest of the trip off.
Luck would once again be on our side however, as we had a great tailwind and covered the remaining distance relatively quickly. Experiments in raising our towels as sails proved to be less effective than just paddling lazily and we checked out two sites, the easterly one where Stefan only slightly fell in the water, and a westerly one. We chose the latter, a raised campsite across from Sandy Inlet with great views of the Inlet, Pickerel Bay, and Mount Napoleon.

We reached the campsite and set up the tents.
Breaking out the goggles we dove into the clear waters and saw some fish among the rocks, they were curious and swam up to us, but didn’t come nearer than half a metre. The drop off to the west also proved interesting as the sunny surface rocks gave way to dark impregnable waters below.



Dinner was ramen with onions, garlic, and the included sauce, which proved so spicy that Graeme had to get out a towel. He and Hudson ended up having to make some rice sides instead. After dinner, with the tents set up we watched the sun light the sky pink and then play that colour all across the waters of Ferguson Bay. Shortly after sundown some coyotes made themselves known as their howls carried across the water.



Euchre in the tent (triumphant victory by Stefan and Jonah) followed sitting by the fire and we retired to sleep, wary of the long journey back that lay ahead of us, but happy to be on the lake, in camp, and together for one last night.

Day 6 | Return | Monday 29.08.2022
Drive: 478km
Paddle: 1.78km
Portage: 1100m


We woke to the rain dowsing the tents, being still too early to do anything about it we rolled over and went back to sleep. When dawn truly came the rain had lessened somewhat and Ferguson Bay was beset by a heavy, driving fog that drifted through the trees like thick smoke.
We took down the tents in the rain and, anxious to get going, forewent breakfast.



A short paddle to the takeout and a long portage to the car brought us to the moment of truth. Had the spare tire survived the week inflated?
To our collective relief it had and after packing the car full of gear, tying the canoes on top, we set off on one of the most nerve-wracking drives of our lives. Behind every bump, every stone, and every puddle lay a flat tire in waiting, and a much more arduous journey home.
The road, being damp from the rain, was dustless, but if anything this only encouraged the logging truckers to drive more recklessly.

Having survived and dropped off our rentals at Smoothwater we drove to Temagami to get the rather squished-looking spare pumped up. A very nice man at the Temagami Auto Clinic let us use his compressor so we could pump it up from 160 to 420kPa (23 to 60psi), where it should have been the whole time. The drive that followed can only be described as long. Rolling along at 80kph while absolutely everyone else overtakes you makes everything seem slower.

At last we made Toronto and after emptying the car we went our separate ways, home to a proper shower and a proper bed.
